TARGET ORDER (mandatory)
================================================================
{{TARGET_ORDER}}

ORDER DEFINITIONS:

first_order:
  Single-step recall. The vignette gives the reader enough discriminating
  clues that the reader can name the diagnosis, drug, anatomic structure,
  pathologic mechanism, etc. directly. Question prompt typically:
  "Which of the following is the most likely diagnosis?",
  "Which structure is most likely affected?",
  "Which mechanism best explains these findings?".
  Stem length: ~5-8 sentences.

second_order:
  Diagnosis is implicit. The reader must first work out the diagnosis,
  THEN apply one additional clinical decision (next best step in
  management, most appropriate next investigation, contraindicated
  drug, prophylactic measure, screening recommendation). Question
  prompt typically:
  "Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in
   management?",
  "Which of the following is the most appropriate initial therapy?",
  "Which of the following is the most appropriate next diagnostic
   test?".
  Stem length: ~6-10 sentences.

third_order:
  Multi-step layered reasoning. The reader must (1) recognize the
  diagnosis or clinical syndrome, (2) integrate comorbidities or
  evolving clinical data (failed first-line, worsening course,
  contraindication, complication), AND (3) select a decision that
  accounts for both. Typically includes: a treatment already given,
  a complication on top of the underlying disease, a comorbidity that
  changes management, a sequential decision after initial intervention,
  or a "what now" after a clinical update mid-stem. Question prompt:
  "Which of the following is the most appropriate next step in
   management?",
  "Which of the following best explains this patient's deterioration?",
  "Which of the following is most appropriate at this time?".
  Stem length: ~8-12 sentences. May include a follow-up update like
  "Two hours later..." or "Despite initial management...".

TARGET DIFFICULTY (mandatory)
================================================================
{{TARGET_DIFFICULTY}}

DIFFICULTY DEFINITIONS:

easy:
  The discriminating clue in the stem is unambiguous (classic clinical
  picture, hallmark lab finding, pathognomonic exam sign). A well-
  prepared M3 would arrive at the answer without struggle. The stem
  still must be realistic and information-dense, but no subtle traps.

medium:
  At least one subtle differentiator (e.g. atypical presentation, mild
  comorbidity that nudges management, distractor diagnosis that shares
  most features but has one discriminating clue, contraindication that
  rules out the most obvious answer). A M4 should get it right with
  thought; a M3 may be tripped.

difficult:
  Multiple integrated clues that REQUIRE the reader to weigh competing
  possibilities. May include: failed initial management, atypical lab
  pattern, comorbid disease that changes the standard answer,
  contraindication for the textbook first-line choice, evolving
  presentation that points away from the obvious first-pass diagnosis.
  A strong M4 / intern should get it; weaker readers go for the
  tempting wrong answer.

Difficulty must come from LAYERED REASONING, MANAGEMENT NUANCE,
COMORBIDITY INTEGRATION, or SUBTLE DIFFERENTIATORS — never from
obscurity. A "rare zebra" is forbidden if the test source is a
high-yield IM/peds/etc. lecture.

================================================================
STEM STYLE — NBME / UWORLD AUTHENTICITY
================================================================
- 5-12 sentences (target per the ORDER definitions above).
- Real clinical voice. Start with demographics + chief complaint
  ("A 67-year-old man comes to the emergency department because of
  crushing substernal chest pain for the past 2 hours...").
- Include vitals when relevant. Include exam findings.
- Include lab values when the question requires them (with units and
  reference ranges where applicable; use NBME-style formatting).
- Comorbidities and medications should be realistic and consistent
  with the demographic + chief complaint.
- For management / third-order questions: include the treatment
  already given AND the clinical response so far.
- Final sentence must be a clear question ending in "?".
- DO NOT include the answer choices.
- DO NOT include any of these forbidden styles:
    "Which of the following is true?"
    "All of the following EXCEPT..."
    "Which of the following best fits..."
    Buzzword-only stems ("What is the most likely diagnosis?\n
     Patient with arthritis + butterfly rash.")
    Trivia-style "what is X" prompts.
