HEART FAILURE — DIVINE INTERVENTION STUDY CONTENT

CLASSIFICATION OF HEART FAILURE

Heart failure with reduced ejection fraction (HFrEF):
- EF < 40%
- Causes: ischemic cardiomyopathy (most common in the US), dilated cardiomyopathy, myocarditis, alcohol cardiomyopathy
- Mechanism: systolic dysfunction — ventricle cannot contract effectively

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF):
- EF ≥ 50%
- Causes: hypertension (most common), diabetes, obesity, atrial fibrillation, aging
- Mechanism: diastolic dysfunction — ventricle cannot relax to fill properly
- HFpEF is increasingly recognized as the more common form in elderly patients

CLINICAL PRESENTATION

Symptoms of heart failure (both HFrEF and HFpEF):
- Dyspnea on exertion: earliest and most common symptom — volume overload increases pulmonary venous pressure
- Orthopnea: dyspnea when lying flat — venous return increases, worsening pulmonary edema. Ask: "How many pillows do you sleep on?"
- Paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND): waking up at night gasping — fluid redistributes from legs to thorax during recumbency
- Peripheral edema: bilateral pitting edema in ankles and lower extremities
- Fatigue and exercise intolerance: low cardiac output → poor skeletal muscle perfusion
- Weight gain: 2-3 lbs/day from fluid retention is a reliable symptom

Right-sided heart failure signs (elevated CVP):
- JVD (jugular venous distension): CVP > 8 cm H2O
- Hepatomegaly and RUQ tenderness: hepatic congestion
- Ascites: portal hypertension from hepatic congestion
- Lower extremity edema

Left-sided heart failure signs (pulmonary congestion):
- Crackles (rales): bilateral basal, from pulmonary edema
- S3 gallop: most specific sign for systolic dysfunction — ventricular filling sound in volume-overloaded, dilated ventricle
- Displaced point of maximal impulse (PMI): lateral and downward, from cardiomegaly

DIAGNOSIS

BNP and NT-proBNP (most important initial test):
- BNP > 100 pg/mL: strongly supports heart failure
- BNP < 100 pg/mL: makes heart failure UNLIKELY (high negative predictive value — use to rule out HF in undifferentiated dyspnea)
- NT-proBNP thresholds: age-adjusted (> 125 pg/mL for < 75 years; > 1800 pg/mL for > 75 years)
- Key clinical use: BNP distinguishes cardiac dyspnea (HF) from pulmonary dyspnea (COPD, asthma, PE)
- BNP is also LOWER in obese patients — adjust interpretation

Echocardiogram (required before treatment):
- Defines EF → distinguishes HFrEF vs HFpEF
- Assesses wall motion abnormalities, valvular disease, pericardial effusion
- Must be performed before initiating evidence-based therapy

Chest X-ray findings in decompensated heart failure:
- Cardiomegaly: cardiothoracic ratio > 0.50 (most common finding)
- Cephalization of pulmonary vasculature: upper lobe vein dilation (early finding)
- Kerley B lines: interstitial edema (horizontal lines at lung bases)
- Bat-wing / butterfly pulmonary edema: alveolar edema radiating from hila (late, severe finding)
- Pleural effusions: typically bilateral, right > left

TREATMENT OF HFrEF — GUIDELINE-DIRECTED MEDICAL THERAPY (GDMT)

Four pillars of GDMT with proven mortality benefit:

1. ARNI (angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor)
   - Sacubitril/valsartan (Entresto) — first-line, superior to ACE inhibitor alone
   - Alternative if ARNI not tolerated: ACE inhibitor (lisinopril, enalapril) or ARB (losartan)
   - Mechanism: ARB blocks angiotensin II + neprilysin inhibitor prevents BNP degradation
   - KEY PEARL: Do NOT use ARNI with ACE inhibitor (angioedema risk). Wait 36 hours after stopping ACE before starting ARNI.

2. Beta-blocker
   - Only three have evidence-based mortality benefit: carvedilol, metoprolol succinate (Toprol XL), bisoprolol
   - Carvedilol: also has alpha-blocking activity (useful with hypertension)
   - Metoprolol tartrate (immediate release) does NOT have the same evidence — use succinate (ER)
   - PARADOX: contraindicated in acute decompensation, REQUIRED for chronic stable HFrEF
   - Start only after patient is euvolemic — starting during fluid overload worsens prognosis

3. Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonist (MRA)
   - Spironolactone or eplerenone
   - Eplerenone: fewer anti-androgenic side effects (gynecomastia, sexual dysfunction)
   - CONTRAINDICATIONS: eGFR < 30, K+ > 5.0 mEq/L, creatinine > 2.5 (women) or > 2.0 (men)
   - Monitor potassium carefully — hyperkalemia risk, especially with ACE/ARB

4. SGLT2 inhibitor (newest pillar, added ~2019-2022)
   - Dapagliflozin (Farxiga) or empagliflozin (Jardiance)
   - Benefit in BOTH HFrEF and HFpEF (unlike beta-blockers which are HFrEF only)
   - Mechanism in HF: osmotic diuresis, reduced preload/afterload, possible direct cardiac effects
   - Also reduces HF hospitalization even in patients without diabetes
   - CONTRAINDICATION: eGFR < 25 (dapagliflozin), eGFR < 20 (empagliflozin)

Diuretics — symptomatic relief only (NO mortality benefit):
- Furosemide (loop diuretic): first-line for volume overload
- Goal: relieve congestion, achieve dry weight
- Monitor: electrolytes (hypokalemia, hyponatremia), renal function, uric acid

DEVICES FOR HFrEF

ICD (implantable cardioverter-defibrillator):
- Indication: EF ≤ 35% despite GDMT × 3 months, NYHA Class II-III
- Rationale: HFrEF patients die of sudden cardiac death (ventricular arrhythmia)
- 3-month waiting period required — EF may improve with GDMT before device placement

CRT (cardiac resynchronization therapy) / CRT-D (with defibrillator):
- Indication: EF ≤ 35% + QRS ≥ 150 ms (ideally ≥ 130 ms) + LBBB morphology, NYHA Class II-III
- Mechanism: re-synchronizes LV and RV contractions, improves EF and symptoms
- Most benefit when LBBB is present (versus RBBB or non-specific IVCD)

TREATMENT OF HFpEF

No single therapy proven to reduce mortality (unlike HFrEF):
- Control underlying conditions: HTN (critical — goal SBP < 130 mmHg), diabetes, obesity, afib
- Diuretics for symptomatic congestion
- SGLT2 inhibitors: best current evidence (EMPEROR-Preserved trial — modest benefit)
- Rate control for atrial fibrillation is crucial
- Beta-blockers: NOT indicated in pure HFpEF (unlike HFrEF)

ACUTE DECOMPENSATED HEART FAILURE (ADHF) MANAGEMENT

Immediate priorities:
- IV furosemide: double the oral home dose for IV equivalent (40mg oral → 40mg IV at minimum)
- Supplemental oxygen; CPAP/BiPAP for respiratory distress
- Fluid restriction
- Position: sit upright (reduces preload)
- Monitor: urine output, daily weights, BMP (renal function + electrolytes)

Vasodilators in ADHF:
- IV nitroglycerin: reduces preload (venodilation) — useful in hypertensive ADHF with pulmonary edema
- IV nitroprusside: reduces both preload and afterload — use with caution (requires arterial line monitoring, risk of cyanide toxicity)

Cardiogenic shock management:
- Inotropes: dobutamine (first-line), milrinone (in beta-blocker users — bypasses beta receptors)
- Vasopressors: norepinephrine for MAP support
- Mechanical circulatory support: IABP, Impella, ECMO for refractory shock

Beta-blocker management in ADHF:
- DO NOT initiate new beta-blocker during acute decompensation
- CONTINUE existing beta-blocker if patient not in cardiogenic shock — abrupt discontinuation worsens outcomes

KEY DISTINGUISHING BOARD PEARLS

- S3 gallop: most specific physical exam finding for HFrEF (low sensitivity, high specificity)
- S4 gallop: found in HFpEF — stiff ventricle requiring forceful atrial kick; also in hypertrophic cardiomyopathy
- BNP < 100: essentially rules out heart failure as cause of dyspnea
- SGLT2 inhibitors: unique in treating BOTH HFrEF and HFpEF — frequently tested
- Metoprolol tartrate vs succinate: only SUCCINATE (ER) has mortality benefit in HF
- Sacubitril/valsartan vs ACE: never combine — 36-hour washout required when switching from ACE
- ICD timing: 3-month window for GDMT optimization before device decision
- HF and diuretics: furosemide treats symptoms but does NOT extend survival
