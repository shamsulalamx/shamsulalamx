* 1. A 67-year-old woman with non-Hodgkin lymphoma is being prepared for discharge from the hospital after treatment of a hypertensive crisis. She is currently receiving her first

course of chemotherapy. One year ago, she received pneumococcal and influenza vaccines. Her blood pressure has responded well to lisinopril. Currently, her temperature is
37°C (98.6°F), pulse is 80/min, respirations are 18/min, and blood pressure is 140/86 mm Hg. Examination shows an enlarged left supraclavicular node. The spleen tip is
palpated 5 cm below the left costal margin. Administration of which of the following vaccines is most appropriate for this patient?

A) Influenza

B) Measles-mumps-rubella
C) Meningococcal

D) Pneumococcal

E) Varicella

Correct Answer: A.

Influenza is a respiratory viral illness that can be complicated by lower respiratory tract infection (pneumonia) and bacterial superinfection, especially in elderly or
immunocompromised patients. Symptoms include fever, chills, myalgias, malaise, headaches, cough, nausea, and vomiting. The Centers for Disease Control (CDC) recommends
annual influenza virus vaccination beginning at age greater than 6 months. The influenza vaccine is available as a trivalent or quadrivalent intramuscular injection of inactivated
virus or as a nasal spray of live attenuated virus. Inactivated influenza vaccine can be safely administered via intramuscular injection to patients who are immunocompromised, who
may be at higher risk for severe illness and influenza-related complications if they forego vaccination and are infected with influenza. Influenza is a seasonal illness, affecting
patients in winter and early spring. This patient who is immunocompromised should receive annual vaccination for influenza virus.

* 2. A 27-year-old woman comes to the physician because of vaginal discharge and itching for 1 week. She has been sexually active with multiple partners for 3 years, and they
use condoms occasionally. Over the past 3 years, she has had three episodes of candidal vaginitis and three episodes of genital herpes. Pelvic examination shows a white
vaginal discharge with a cottage-cheese consistency. Examination shows no other abnormalities. A Pap smear is obtained. Topical antifungal medications are prescribed.

A) HIV testing
B) Fungal culture
C) Herpes culture
D) Human papillomavirus culture

Correct Answer: A.

one-time screening is recommended for most sexually active individuals over the age of 13 years, but more frequent screening is indicated for those with a higher risk for
contracting HIV. These populations include those with numerous sexual partners, especially if barrier protection is infrequently used, patients who exchange sex for money, injection
drug users, those who have HIV-positive partners, and men who have sex with men, among others. This patient's high-risk sexual behavior and history of recurrent genital herpes

typically involves the use of a fourth-generation combined HIV 1/2 antigen and antibody test. If the test is positive, then further antibody testing to differentiate between infection with
HIV-1 or HIV-2 is indicated. If the fourth-generation test is positive but the results of further antibody testing are negative or indeterminate, then the HIV viral load should be
measured. While genotyping is usually recommended to determine resistance patterns for antiretroviral drugs, a positive HIV test should prompt immediate initiation of highly active
antiretroviral therapy (HAART).

* 3. Six hours after beginning dicloxacillin therapy for cellulitis of the right lower extremity, a 37-year-old woman comes to the emergency department because of a 3-hour history of
pruritic rash, dyspnea, and mild cough. She has a history of childhood asthma but no history of adverse reactions to medication. Six months ago, she had streptococcal
pharyngitis treated with penicillin. She has otherwise been healthy. Her temperature is 37.1°C (98.8°F), pulse is 110/min, respirations are 22/min, and blood pressure is
86/64 mm Hg. Pulse oximetry on room air shows an oxygen saturation of 92%. Examination of the skin shows 1- to 2-cm areas of pink urticaria over the trunk and upper and
lower extremities and a 3-cm, erythematous, mildly tender area over the distal right lower extremity. Air movement is poor, and inspiratory and expiratory wheezes are heard
on auscultation. Her hemoglobin concentration is 13.1 g/dL, and leukocyte count is 9500/mm3. A chest x-ray shows no abnormalities. In addition to discontinuing dicloxacillin
therapy, which of the following is the most appropriate next step in pharmacotherapy?

A) Epinephrine
B) Loratadine
C) Prednisone
D) Stanozolol
Correct Answer: A.

reaction that develops following exposure to an allergen; in this case, the presumed allergen is dicloxacillin. It is caused by widespread IgE-mediated mast cell degranulation and is
characterized by histaminergic responses within multiple organ systems leading to flushing, angioedema, bronchospasm, nausea, vomiting, abdominal discomfort, and vasodilation,

resuscitation, and potentially invasive airway or vasopressor support. When medications are suspected as the cause of anaphylaxis an allergy warning should be placed in the
patient's chart. At the time of discharge, all patients should be provided with an epinephrine autoinjector with instructions on how to use it. They should also be educated on the
nature of their allergy and advised to avoid antibiotics within the same class in the future. In some instances, referral to an allergist may be appropriate.

* 4. A previously healthy 47-year-old man comes to the physician because of a 3-month history of progressively worsening fatigue; he has had a 14-kg (30-Ib) weight loss during
this period. Vital signs are within normal limits. The spleen tip is palpated 8 cm below the left costal margin. Examination shows no other abnormalities. His hematocrit is 40%,
leukocyte count is 36,000/mm%, and platelet count is 70,000/mm3. Bone marrow aspirate and biopsy show 100% cellularity with an increase in all hematopoietic cell lines. The
presence of which of the following is most likely to confirm the diagnosis?

A) BCR/ABL fusion protein

B) BRCA1 oncogene

C) P-glycoprotein

D) p53 Suppressor gene

E) t (8,16) Translocation
Correct Answer: A.

The presence of the BCR/ABL fusion protein, the product of an abnormal chromosome known as the Philadelphia (Ph) chromosome, will likely confirm the diagnosis of chronic
myelogenous leukemia (CML). CML is diagnosed by the presence of the Ph chromosome, a translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22, which causes constitutive activation of
the ABL kinase protein. Activation of ABL kinase leads to subsequent activation of the JAK/STAT and Ras/MAPK/ERK pathways, leading to increased hematopoietic proliferation,
inhibition of normal checkpoint inhibition, and resistance to apoptosis. While the Ph chromosome is required for the diagnosis of CML, it can also be found in acute lymphoblastic
leukemia (ALL) and acute myeloid leukemia (AML). Typical laboratory findings in CML include leukocytosis with an increase in nearly all cell lines. Basophilia and eosinophilia are
characteristic. Leukostasis, a condition in which leukocytosis leads to end organ damage from occlusion of capillaries by malignant cells, is uncommon in CML. This contrasts with
conditions such as acute leukemias in which circulating blasts, which are large and not particularly pliable, easily lodge in capillaries causing pulmonary and neurologic
complications. Treatment of CML is primarily with tyrosine kinase inhibitors such as imatinib or dasatinib. Such medications are effective and often result in complete molecular
response (remission) within several months of initiation.

Incorrect Answers: B, C, D, and E.

The BRCAT7 oncogene (Choice B) is implicated in the development of breast and ovarian cancer. This gene is considered a tumor suppressor gene that, when functioning normally,
aids the repair of damaged DNA. Mutations render this protein nonfunctional, thereby increasing the risk for DNA damage and thus the risk for breast cancer.

P-glycoprotein (Choice C) is an efflux pump that belongs to the ABC class of transporters. Leukemic cells may develop the ability to actively shunt chemotherapeutic agents out of
the cell using p-glycoproteins, thereby rendering them resistant. However, the presence of p-glycoproteins does not confirm the presence of CML.

* 5. A 42-year-old woman comes to the physician for a follow-up examination. She has had paraplegia since being involved in a motor vehicle collision 12 years ago. She has had
recurrent urinary tract infections over the past 10 years; the most recent infection occurred 3 months ago. Examination shows no abnormalities other than paraplegia. Test of
the stool for occult blood is negative. Laboratory studies show:

Hemoglobin 8.7 g/dL
Mean corpuscular volume 90 ums
Leukocyte count 6300/mm3
Platelet count 160,000/mm3

Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's decreased hemoglobin concentration?

A) Cytokine-induced erythropoietin deficiency
B) Folic acid deficiency
C) Glucose 6-phosphate dehydrogenase deficiency
D) Iron deficiency
E) Myelodysplasia
Correct Answer: A.

Cytokine-induced erythropoietin deficiency is the most likely cause of this patient's normocytic anemia, which is commonly referred to as anemia of chronic disease (ACD). ACD is
common in patients with rheumatologic disorders, renal disease, malignancies, and chronic infections, as in this case. Chronic inflammation with release of inflammatory
substances such as interleukin-1 and tumor necrosis factor-a lead to an increase in hepcidin production, a decreased responsiveness of the bone marrow to erythropoietin, and
retention of iron within the reticuloendothelial system (RES). This confluence of factors leads to impaired iron absorption and recycling, with a consequent state of functional iron
deficiency despite adequate iron stores. Iron that is retained within the RES cannot effectively be accessed and used for erythropoiesis, so patients present with either normocytic
or microcytic anemia and reduced numbers of reticulocytes indicating inadequate bone marrow response to anemia. Other laboratory findings include a normal to slightly increased
ferritin concentration, which is the storage form of iron, with decreased serum iron concentration and a decreased total iron-binding capacity (TIBC). Treatment must focus on
treating the underlying disease to decrease inflammation.

Incorrect Answers: B, C, D, and E.

Thalassemia is the most likely diagnosis in this African American patient with mild microcytic anemia with a normal red cell distribution width (RDW) and normal iron studies that
hasn't changed despite several months of iron therapy. Thalassemia is an inherited condition in which one or more alleles of either the a- or B-globin genes is rendered
nonfunctional, thereby interfering with normal synthesis of hemoglobin A (HbA). The normal adult hemoglobin consists of two a-globin subunits and two B-globin subunits which
together form a 8 , (HbA), a unique conformation that has a high affinity for oxygen in the lungs and can offload oxygen effectively in the peripheral tissues. In patients who have
one or more deficient B-globin genes (B-thalassemia major or minor), the normal HbA molecule cannot be effectively assembled, and the excess a-globin chains combine with one
another to form insoluble tetramers. There are four a-globin alleles, and the severity of a-thalassemia depends on the number of these alleles that are non-functional. Many patients
with a-thalassemia trait are asymptomatic and only exhibit minimal decreases in hemoglobin concentration, although microcytosis is frequently encountered. Patients with more
nonfunctional alleles present with increasingly severe levels of anemia. Microcytosis and the presence of target cells on peripheral blood smear are characteristic of both a- and
B-thalassemia. These two conditions can be reliably distinguished using hemoglobin electrophoresis. Patients with thalassemia trait or thalassemia minor are often incorrectly
diagnosed as having iron deficiency anemia, and in these instances, oral iron therapy will have no effect on the hemoglobin concentrations as in this patient. A transferrin saturation
of 18 to 20% is often used to exclude iron deficiency anemia.

* 26. A 57-year-old woman comes to the physician because of a 10-month history of severe fatigue. She awakens four to five times nightly to void. She now takes daily naps and
frequently falls asleep while watching television. She has noted swelling of her legs and occasional palpitations during the past 6 months. She has not had fever, night
sweats, or change in bowel movements. She has had an 18-kg (40-Ib) weight gain during the past year. She is 160 cm (5 ft 3 in) tall and now weighs 145 kg (320 Ib); BMI is
57 kg/m2. Her pulse is 96/min, respirations are 20/min, and blood pressure is 160/95 mm Hg. Pulse oximetry on room air shows an oxygen saturation of 92%. An x-ray of the
chest shows no abnormalities. An ECG shows sinus tachycardia and P pulmonale. Which of the following is the most likely cause of this patient's nocturia?

A) Chronic obstructive pulmonary disease

B) Cystitis

C) Hypothyroidism

D) Narcolepsy

E) Obesity-hypoventilation syndrome
Correct Answer: E.

Obesity-hypoventilation syndrome (OHS) is the most likely cause of this patient's nocturia. OHS is occasionally encountered in patients with a BMI over 30 kg/m2, and it is common
in patients with a BMI over 50 kg/m2. Chronic alveolar hypoventilation leads to both hypercapnia and hypoxemia. Hypercapnia results in a compensatory metabolic alkalosis, which
manifests as an increased serum bicarbonate concentration, while chronic hypoxemia can lead to pulmonary hypertension and polycythemia. When severe, resting hypoxemia may
be apparent. Pulmonary function tests are often done to exclude other causes of hypoventilation and hypercapnia. These studies are frequently normal, although they may disclose
varying degrees of restrictive lung disease and reduced diffusing capacity. There is a substantial overlap with obstructive sleep apnea (OSA) as well, but OSA does not cause
chronic hypercapnia. Because of substantial clinical overlap, it is common for patients to exhibit symptoms of hypersomnolence, morning headaches, fatigue, snoring, and nocturia.
Nocturia is thought to occur because of an increased circulating concentration of atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP), which is either triggered by atrial distention resulting from an
increasingly negative intrathoracic pressure that develops when breathing is occluded during sleep, or from hypoxia-induced pulmonary vasoconstriction. In either case, ANP
inhibits the release of antidiuretic hormone leading to nocturia through increased excretion of free water. The diagnosis of OHS can be made in a patient with a BMI over 30 kg/m2
with documented alveolar hypoventilation while awake after exclusion of competing diagnoses.

A positive likelihood ratio is the likelihood that a patient with the disease will have a positive test result compared to the likelihood that a patient without the disease will have a
positive test result. It can be calculated by dividing the sensitivity by (1-specificity). In contrast, the negative likelihood ratio is the likelihood that a patient with the disease will have a
